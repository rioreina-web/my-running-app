# Post Run Drip — Brand Voice & Messaging

**Purpose:** A single reference for how Post Run Drip talks. Use this for in-app microcopy, marketing content, emails, social posts, landing pages — anywhere the brand speaks.

**Central tension:** you want coaching and AI to feel hand-in-hand, with coaching leading. This document is built around that.

---

## 1. The one-sentence positioning

**A coach that actually knows you.**

That's the brand. Everything else flows from it. *Coach* is the noun — it's what the product is. *Actually knows you* is the promise — grounded in your training, not guessed from averages. *AI* is nowhere in the sentence. That's deliberate.

---

## 2. How AI shows up

Not in the name. Not in the tagline. Not in the headline on the landing page. **AI is the engine; coaching is the face.**

Where AI appears:
- In capability copy, as verbs: *"reads your logs," "adjusts to your actual paces," "checks the forecast for race day."*
- In evidence, never in marketing: *"every recommendation cites the workout it's based on."*
- In skeptic-friendly contexts: *"yes, there's AI under the hood. Here's what we do with it, and here's what we don't."*

Where AI never appears:
- In the headline
- In the tagline
- In app store copy ("AI-powered" is a scam-detector trigger for serious runners)
- In onboarding screens
- In the coach's own voice (the coach never says "As an AI...")

**The test:** if you put your homepage side-by-side with a competitor's and both say "AI-powered" at the top, you've positioned yourself exactly like them. You haven't.

## 3. Tone attributes

Six attributes, ordered by importance. Every piece of copy should pass all six.

### 3.1 Coach first, software second

Speak like a coach. Direct, specific, knowledgeable, kind. The app exists; don't make it the subject of every sentence.

- Wrong: *"The app analyzes your workouts and suggests..."*
- Right: *"Your last three thresholds came in 4s under target. Time to bump intervals."*

### 3.2 Grounded, not generic

Every claim, every recommendation, every piece of marketing copy should reference something specific — a workout, a metric, a condition, a block. Generic fitness language is banned.

- Wrong: *"Crush your next race."*
- Right: *"The weather-adjusted pace for Saturday's threshold is 6:12–6:18."*

### 3.3 Restrained, not robotic (and not hyped)

Two failure modes — AI-speak and fitness-bro-speak. Avoid both.

- Banned AI-speak: *"I notice that," "Feel free to," "Let me know if," "I'd be happy to," "Based on your data,"* "That's a great question."*
- Banned bro-speak: *"grind," "journey," "crush," "beast mode," "go hard," "champion," "unleash," "transform,"* "warrior."*
- Runner-speak is fine: *"easy miles," "long run," "threshold," "splits," "negative split," "PR," "block."* Those are real words runners use.

### 3.4 Honest when uncertain

The single biggest trust-builder. The coach admits what it doesn't know.

- *"I don't have enough data to answer that specifically yet."*
- *"Your pace profile is fresh from last week — high confidence."*
- *"This is a prediction, not a race result — I could be off."*
- *"Your last three weeks have been light on quality, so I'm guessing on fitness."*

This is uncommon in AI products and it's why it works.

### 3.5 Peer energy, not authority energy

Runner-to-runner, not coach-to-student. You're not telling them what to do; you're sharing what you see and making a call together.

- Wrong: *"You need to do your threshold workout today."*
- Right: *"Tuesday's threshold is up. You feeling it or should we move it?"*

The voice is: experienced friend who happens to be a coach, not a clinician.

### 3.6 Numbers over adjectives

If the app or marketing says *"you're improving,"* it should be immediately backed by a number. Adjectives without specifics are noise.

- Wrong: *"Your fitness is trending up."*
- Right: *"Your 10K prediction dropped 14s in the last four weeks."*

## 4. Messaging hierarchy

Use this order in landing pages, long-form content, and investor/partner decks.

1. **Hook:** *A coach that actually knows you.*
2. **Problem:** Generic AI coaches give generic advice. You're not a generic runner.
3. **Solution:** A coach that reads your workouts, your paces, your weather, your week — and adjusts.
4. **Differentiation:** Every recommendation cites the data behind it. If there isn't enough data, the coach says so. No hallucination, no hedging, no hype.
5. **Proof:** Weather-adjusted pace targets. Pace zones that update when you PR. Plans that actually move when your life does.
6. **Call to action:** Direct. Not "start your journey." Something like *"Run with us"* or *"Try it for a week"* or just *"Open the app."*

---

## 5. The "AI-and-coaching-hand-in-hand" positioning — in practice

The message isn't *"AI + human coaching"* (you don't have a human coach in the loop). It's *"the discipline and voice of a great coach, implemented with AI."*

Three sentences that land this:

> The coach reads your training the way a good human coach would. Knows which thresholds were hard, which felt easy, which you skipped. And like a good coach, it admits when it's guessing.

> We built the software to do the boring work — reading your logs, tracking your paces, watching the weather — so the coaching can focus on the decisions. When to push, when to recover, when to move the long run.

> If the data says you're improving, we tell you. If it doesn't, we don't pretend. That honesty is what AI coaching usually lacks.

Use one of these (or a variation) anywhere you need to explain the product in a paragraph.

## 7. Channel-specific guidance

### In-app microcopy

Shorter is better. Never more than one sentence where half a sentence works.

- Empty state: *"No workouts yet. Log one and I'll have something to say."*
- Error: *"Couldn't reach the coach. Try again in a minute."*
- Loading: *"Thinking about your week..."*
- Success: *"Done."* (not "Successfully saved!")

### Instagram

Runner-to-runner. Iphone photos are fine. Captions are short, specific, occasionally wry. Never stock-photo energy.

- *"Weather-adjusted pace for this morning: 7:05. Actual: 6:58. Coach says good week."*
- *"Every AI running app promises it'll know you. We decided to actually do it."*

### Email

Subject lines are declarative, not teasing. Body opens with a specific observation.

- Subject: *"Your pace profile just updated."* (not *"Big news inside!"*)
- Opening: *"You logged three workouts last week. Here's what the coach picked up."*

### Reddit (r/AdvancedRunning)

**Never self-promotional.** Genuine technical contributions with a quiet link in the comments if asked. The subreddit will sniff out marketing energy in milliseconds.

- Good post angle: *"We built a dew-point-adjusted pace calculator for our running app — here's the formula and why we chose it over [alternative]."*
- Bad post angle: *"Check out our new AI running coach!"*

### Landing page

Opens with the hook sentence. No hero video. No "trusted by" logos. A screenshot of the product doing something specific and useful is the headline image. Everything else is in service of that.

## 8. Coach-in-app voice specifics

When the in-app coach talks to the user, it must follow these rules (this layer already partially exists in `coaching-agent/index.ts`):

- **Never invent data.** Only reference workouts/paces/stats that are in the context.
- **Always cite specifics.** "Last Tuesday's threshold" beats "your recent workouts."
- **Admit uncertainty openly.** "Not enough data yet" is better than guessing.
- **Push back when appropriate.** If the athlete proposes something risky, the coach says so — not rudely, but clearly.
- **Stay out of the user's life decisions.** It's a running coach. It doesn't comment on their diet, relationships, stress. It can note sleep/mood as training signals, not as things to fix.
- **No methodology name-dropping.** No "Jack Daniels says," no "Pfitzinger plan," no "according to sports science." The coach is the authority, not an attribution engine.
- **Never tell the athlete to push through pain.** A hard-coded safety rule that overrides everything else.

## 9. What the brand is NOT

As important as what it is.

- **Not a generic fitness app.** This isn't Peloton, it isn't Noom, it isn't a wellness product. Serious runners, training for real times.
- **Not a social network.** Strava already won that. The app doesn't try to be a feed.
- **Not a chatbot.** The coaching layer isn't a novelty. It's a working coach with structured reasoning behind it.
- **Not a gimmick.** No streaks, no badges, no "unlock your inner runner" framing. Numbers matter. Outcomes matter. Vibes don't.
- **Not cheap.** Serious runners pay for serious tools. The app should feel worth paying for.
- **Not for beginners.** This isn't Couch to 5K. Messaging should be comfortable saying "if you're new to running, this probably isn't the right app yet" — that's brand-strengthening, not customer-losing.

---

## 10. When in doubt

Read the copy out loud.

If it sounds like something your running friends would say → ship it.
If it sounds like something a marketing team wrote about running → rewrite.
If it sounds like an AI wrote it → definitely rewrite.
