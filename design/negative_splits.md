# Negative Splits — A Visual Philosophy for Running Log

## I. Manifesto

A *negative split* is when the second half is faster than the first. It is the runner's expression of restraint paying off — patience held, then released. This philosophy carries that same shape into design: open quietly, build with confidence, finish with intent. The work begins in restraint and lets meaning accelerate. Nothing announces itself before it must.

The result is not minimalism for its own sake. Minimalism, badly practiced, looks like an absence of decisions. *Negative Splits* is the opposite — every absence is a decision, made by someone who could have done more and chose not to. Each tile of negative space is the product of painstaking attention, the kind a designer at the top of their craft applies before deleting half of what they made. The viewer should feel the labor as a quiet confidence, never as decoration.

## II. Form & Space

The canvas breathes. Generous margins. Unfilled rectangles. The first impression should be of air, not information. Hierarchy emerges from spatial gesture before it emerges from size or weight — what is highest on the page leads, what is set apart commands. Grids are honored but never visible; the eye should sense the underlying order without being shown the rulebook. Compositions favor a tall vertical rhythm, broken occasionally by a single horizontal element — the finish line rendered as a thin rule.

## III. Color & Material

A palette of three working colors and one accent. The working colors live in the warm-cool middle: bone, ink, and a soft slate that feels like dawn fog over a track. The accent is reserved — a single signal color, used only where the eye must land. (A heart-rate orange. A late-afternoon amber.) Color carries information; it is never ornament. Backgrounds remain neutral and matte, as though printed on uncoated paper. The accent appears no more than once or twice per composition. Restraint here is the entire point — when the accent does appear, it must be earned.

## IV. Typography & Number

Letters whisper. Numbers sing. Body text and labels recede into a thin sans-serif at small sizes. The numerals — pace, distance, ratio, week count — are given the dignity normally reserved for chapter openings: a generous serif or display face, set in a size that asks the reader to slow down and look. Typography is treated as a tonal instrument; the contrast between hushed labels and loud figures creates the rhythm of the page. Tabular figures, monospaced where data aligns. No italics for emphasis — emphasis comes from scale and air.

## V. Composition & Rhythm

The eye is led down the page in three movements: an opening figure that invites; a middle passage where data accumulates patiently; a closing line — sometimes literal, sometimes typographic — that resolves. Repeating elements (small markers, ticks, micro-lines) appear in regular intervals, building the sense that this is the artifact of a discipline, recorded over time. Pages may carry a faint annotation in the margin: a date, a coordinate, a tempo marking — the way a researcher labels a plate, the way a coach scribbles a split.

## VI. The Test

Each composition should pass three tests. *Could it be reduced further?* If yes, reduce. *Does anything appear by default rather than by decision?* If yes, remove. *Does the work look as though it took countless hours?* It must. The mark of master-level execution here is invisibility of effort — the page should feel inevitable, as though it could not have been arranged otherwise. The viewer should sense the hand of someone who knows the discipline cold and has chosen, deliberately, to say less.

This is design that runs the second half faster.
